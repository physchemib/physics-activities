Forces in Air

Air may seem invisible and easy to overlook, but it can exert important forces on moving objects. When an object travels through air, the interaction between its shape, speed and the surrounding airflow can change the pressure around it and produce forces such as lift and drag. In engineering and physics, understanding these forces helps explain why some objects rise, why some slow down, and why force balance is so important for steady motion. This page introduces the key ideas in a clear way: lift, drag, thrust, weight, pressure differences and a few essential equations.

Introduction

Objects moving through air experience more than just motion in a straight line. The air around them can push, resist and redirect their motion. In many situations, we describe this using a small set of forces: upward lift, downward weight, forward thrust and backward drag.

A useful starting point is to think about force balance. If upward and downward forces balance, there is no vertical acceleration. If forward and backward forces balance, the object does not speed up or slow down horizontally. If the forces do not balance, acceleration occurs.

One of the most interesting ideas is that moving air can create pressure differences. These pressure differences can lead to upward force. At the same time, motion through air also produces drag, which resists movement. Together, these ideas help explain how motion in air can be analysed.

The main forces

When an object moves through air, several forces can act on it at the same time. In simple flight models, the four most common forces are lift, weight, thrust and drag.

Lift

Upward force caused by air acting on a moving surface.

Weight

Downward force due to gravity. Near Earth, \(W = mg\).

Thrust

Forward force that pushes an object through the air.

Drag

Resistive force that opposes motion through a fluid.

Key idea: motion depends on the net force. If forces balance, motion stays steady. If they do not balance, the object accelerates.

Essential equations

\(W = mg\)
Weight near the Earth’s surface.
  • \(W\) = weight force
  • \(m\) = mass
  • \(g\) = gravitational field strength
\(L = W\)
Condition for steady vertical motion or hovering.
  • \(L\) = lift force
  • \(W\) = weight force
\(T = D\)
Condition for constant horizontal speed.
  • \(T\) = thrust force
  • \(D\) = drag force
\(F_d = 6 \pi \eta r v\)
Low-speed viscous drag model for a small sphere in a fluid.
  • \(F_d\) = drag force
  • \(\eta\) = viscosity of the fluid
  • \(r\) = radius of the sphere
  • \(v\) = speed of the sphere through the fluid
\(F_b = \rho V g\)
Buoyant force in a fluid.
  • \(F_b\) = buoyant force
  • \(\rho\) = density of the fluid
  • \(V\) = volume of fluid displaced
  • \(g\) = gravitational field strength

Lift and pressure

In a simplified model, air can move faster over the top of a curved surface and more slowly below it. This can lead to a pressure difference, with lower pressure above and higher pressure below, producing an upward force.

At IB level, it is useful to remember the main idea: pressure differences in a fluid can create a net upward force.

Keep in mind: lift is often explained using pressure differences and airflow, but force balance is still the most important starting point for this page.

Drag and viscosity

Drag is the force that opposes motion through a fluid such as air. In general, drag becomes larger when speed increases. Shape also matters: streamlined objects usually experience less drag.

Viscosity describes a fluid’s resistance to flow. In some models, especially for small spheres moving slowly in a fluid, viscous drag can be described mathematically using Stokes’ law.

Simple trend: faster motion through air usually means greater resistance.

Keywords

Lift

Upward force caused by air acting on a moving object.

Drag

Force that opposes motion through air.

Thrust

Forward force that drives motion through air.

Weight

Downward force caused by gravity.

Pressure

Force per unit area exerted by a fluid.

Airflow

The movement of air around an object.

Viscosity

How much a fluid resists flowing.

Buoyancy

Upward force caused by pressure differences in a fluid.

Net force

The overall force after all forces are combined.

Reading the graph

The simulation graph compares the coefficient of lift and the coefficient of drag as the angle of attack changes. A higher lift coefficient means the wing is producing more lift relative to the conditions, while a higher drag coefficient means more resistance through the air.

A useful pattern to notice is that lift increases only up to a point. After that, the graph may show a sudden drop in lift, while drag does not improve. This means that making the angle of attack larger is not always better.
